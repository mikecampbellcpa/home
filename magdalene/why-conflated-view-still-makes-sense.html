Mary Magdalene and Mary, Sister of Lazarus & Martha
Why the Unified Identity Still Makes Sense

For most of the Church’s history, the Latin tradition held a simple and elegant conviction: Mary Magdalene, Mary the sister of Martha and Lazarus, and the woman who anointed Jesus in Luke 7 are the same person. This was not a medieval accident, nor a pious confusion. It was the consensus of saints, pastors, theologians, and ordinary Christians for well over 1,600 years.

A Tradition Rooted in the Early Church

Beginning in the 300s, figures such as Ambrose, Augustine, Gregory the Great, Bede, Bernard, and Aquinas spoke of Mary Magdalene and Mary, Sister of Lazarus & Martha as one woman. This is not a minor footnote in patristic literature — it is the dominant Western reading of the Gospels. And importantly, this tradition did not suddenly appear in the fourth century. It reflects earlier oral memory carried into the Latin Church and harmonized with the Gospel narratives.

When a tradition is that old, that stable, and that widely held, it deserves serious respect. The Church did not lightly conflate major biblical figures. The Fathers believed they were preserving something received, not inventing something new.

The French Apostolic Memory

The ancient Provençal tradition strengthens this picture. In the earliest Christian memory of southern Gaul, Lazarus is remembered as the first bishop of Marseille, Martha as an evangelist in Tarascon, and Mary Magdalene as the contemplative who lived in the caves of Sainte-Baume. Crucially, these traditions present all three as siblings who arrived together after the Ascension.

This is not a modern legend. It is one of the oldest Western apostolic traditions, woven into the identity of the local Church in France and preserved for centuries. It assumes — without hesitation — that Mary Magdalene and Mary, Sister of Lazarus & Martha are the same woman.

Textual Coherence: One Story, Not Three

When we read the Gospels with the whole story in view, the unified identity is not only plausible — it is narratively compelling.

Across the four Gospels, we encounter a woman who:

  • Sits at Jesus’s feet
  • Anoints His feet
  • Loves Him with unusual depth
  • Experiences profound conversion
  • Stays at the Cross when others flee
  • Meets Him at the Tomb
  • Becomes the first witness of the Resurrection

It is difficult to imagine the evangelists intending three different women to perform the same symbolic actions, embody the same spiritual posture, and occupy the same narrative arc. The simpler reading — and the ancient reading — is that these threads belong to one woman whose life was transformed by Christ.

“Magdalene” Is Not a Barrier

The title “Magdalene” does not prove a separate identity. People in Scripture often have multiple geographic associations. Mary could easily be known as “Magdalene” because of family origin, marriage, property, or extended kinship — just as Mary and Elizabeth lived in different towns despite close family ties.

It means nothing that Martha and Lazarus live in Bethany while Mary is called Magdalene. The Gospels never say she lived in Magdala — only that she was called Magdalene.

A Modern Separation Without New Evidence

The separation of these figures in the Latin Church is extremely recent. It entered popular consciousness only in the 1960s, driven not by new archaeological discoveries or manuscript evidence, but by a shift toward historical-critical interpretation. No new artifact overturned the ancient tradition. Only the hermeneutical lens changed.

For catechists, this matters. When a long-standing tradition is set aside without new historical data, it is worth asking whether the older reading still offers theological, pastoral, and narrative coherence.

Why the Unified Identity Still Matters

Seeing Mary Magdalene and Mary, Sister of Lazarus & Martha as one woman gives us a portrait of discipleship that is both beautiful and complete: A woman forgiven much, who loved much, who listened at His feet, who anointed Him for burial, who stayed at the Cross, and who proclaimed the Resurrection.

It is a single arc of redemption, devotion, and mission — the kind of story early Christians would have remembered, cherished, and passed down.

The unified identity is not only historically defensible. It is spiritually fitting.

Feast Days of Mary Magdalene and Mary, Martha, & Lazarus

Mary Magdalene began to be commemorated liturgically in Rome on July 22nd from the 11th century, with observance spreading to other parts of Western Europe during the 12th century. In 2016, Pope Francis elevated the liturgical rank of St. Mary Magdalene’s celebration from a Memorial to a Feast, highlighting her role as apostle to the apostles.

Martha had her own feast day for over 1300 years until in 2021, the memorial of July 29 was expanded to include her siblings Mary and Lazarus. This was the first time that Lazarus had a feast day on the Western calendar.

Neither change altered Mary Magdalene’s feast day nor constituted a magisterial separation from Mary, Sister of Martha & Lazarus.
